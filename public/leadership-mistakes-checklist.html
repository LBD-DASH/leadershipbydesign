The 5 Leadership Mistakes Costing You Your Best Employees

A practical checklist for leaders who want to retain top talent

Most leaders unknowingly make at least 2-3 of these mistakes regularly. The good news? Once you're aware of them, they're surprisingly simple to fix. Use this checklist to identify your blind spots and take immediate action.

1

Delegating Tasks, Not Outcomes

Why It Happens

You're busy and it's faster to give specific instructions. But when you assign tasks without explaining the "why," you turn capable professionals into order-takers.

The Fix

Share the outcome you need and the constraints, then let them figure out the how. "I need the client presentation ready by Thursday that addresses their concerns about timeline" beats "Make 15 slides about our process."

When was the last time you delegated an outcome rather than a to-do list?

2

Waiting for Annual Reviews to Give Feedback

Why It Happens

Formal review cycles feel like the "right" time for feedback. But your best employees want to grow now, not once a year. Delayed recognition feels hollow.

The Fix

Make feedback a weekly habit, not an annual event. A 30-second acknowledgment in the moment is worth more than a paragraph in a performance review three months later.

💭

Who on your team did something noteworthy this week that you haven't acknowledged yet?

3

Solving Problems Your Team Should Own

Why It Happens

You know the answer, so why waste time? But every problem you solve is a development opportunity stolen from your team—and it creates dependency.

The Fix

When someone brings you a problem, respond with "What do you think we should do?" Even if you need to course-correct, you're building their capability, not their reliance on you.

💭

How many problems did you solve this week that someone on your team could have handled?

4

Having an "Open Door" But a Closed Mind

Why It Happens

You genuinely believe you're approachable. But if people rarely bring you bad news or challenge your ideas, your "openness" might be performative, not real.

The Fix

Actively seek dissent. Ask "What am I missing?" and "What would you do differently?" Then—crucially—listen without defending. Your reaction to pushback determines whether people will ever push back again.

💭

When was the last time someone on your team disagreed with you openly?

5

Focusing on What's Wrong, Not What's Strong

Why It Happens

Problems demand attention. Weaknesses feel urgent to fix. But your best people want to grow their strengths, not just patch their gaps—and research shows strength-based development is 6x more engaging.

The Fix

Spend more time amplifying what people do brilliantly than correcting what they struggle with. Ask "How can we give you more opportunities to use this strength?" rather than "How do we fix this weakness?"

💭

Can you name each team member's top strength? When did you last create an opportunity for them to use it?

📋 Your Action Checklist

  • This week, I'll delegate an outcome instead of a task list
  • I'll give one piece of in-the-moment feedback within 24 hours
  • When someone brings me a problem, I'll ask "What do you think?" first
  • I'll actively ask for one dissenting opinion in my next meeting
  • I'll identify one team member's strength and find a way to amplify it
